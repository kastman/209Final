1) Problem Statement and Motivation: This should be brief and self-contained.                                                 

Our team was assigned the Crime topic. In line with the class’s emphasis on prediction over inference, our project deals with the question “How well can we predict the level of crime in a city based on its demographic data”? More specifically, our project aims to predict murder rates in Metropolitan Statistical Areas using census data on these areas’ demographic indicators such as age, gender, income, unemployment rate and educational attainment.  

        

2) Introduction and Description of Data: Description of relevant knowledge. Why is this problem important? Why is it challenging? Introduce the motivations for the project question and how that question was defined through preliminary EDA.

Violent crime is a major issue that impacts the social, political and economic aspects of every society, and at least in the United States, it seems to be on the rise. 2016 saw an increase in violent crimes in several large American cities[1]. As the negative effects of crime on our society’s well-being are felt, the idea of predicting and preventing crime becomes increasingly attractive. Can we use the aggregate data we have on past crimes to find patterns in criminal activity and inform more effective crime prevention strategies?

However, crime prediction is not so simple, and comes with major challenges. The available crime data may include severe reporting biases: for example, if the police concentrate on patrolling individuals and communities with specific racial and socioeconomic backgrounds, they will probably find and report more criminal activity in those groups. If we then feed these data to a machine learning algorithm, the model will learn those patterns and predict that those groups are more likely to commit [reported] crimes, which may or may not be true. These predictive models might then be used to inform where the police should concentrate their efforts, further reinforcing these biases[2].

The current project seeks to better understand the available demographic data surrounding murder in metropolitan areas in the United States, and to explore the complexities associated with making predictions based on this data. Specifically, this project used the following datasets:

  • Crime data: We scraped and used data from the FBI website (https://ucr.fbi.gov/ucr-publications) on the number of murders by Metropolitan Statistical Area from 2006 to 2016. Data from the FBI crime database is provided as a set of pdfs or websites, and we scraped the html sites as suggested by the problem statement. However, there are several edge cases which must be accounted for, including different html formatting for the tables themselves (sometimes header cells are <th> tags, for example, and sometimes they’re <td> tags, which changes column positions, etc.).

  • Demographic data: We took demographic data from the American Community Survey available on the website of the United States Census Bureau (https://factfinder.census.gov/faces/nav/jsf/pages/index.xhtml). More specifically, we downloaded and put together the four “Data Profile” tables offered on the website for each year from 2007 through 2016. These “Data Profiles” contained measures of interest on social, economic, housing, and demographic data. Each of these four subject areas is offered as a separate table for each year from 2007-2016, with each table containing a separate row for each MSA included.  Assembling all of these tables into one dataframe presented some challenges. Most notably, although the questions asked remain consistent from year to year, the question names are formatted differently in each table. For instance, the same question may be entered as “Percent; Estimate; HOUSEHOLDS BY TYPE - Total households - Male householder, no wife present, family” in one year and “Percent; HOUSEHOLDS BY TYPE - Total households - Family households (families) - Male householder, no wife present, family” in another. Furthermore, some questions enter or leave the dataset in certain years.

To address the ACS formatting issues, we built a table mapping each as-written column name to a finalized column name. Though there are far too many questions to process by hand, each raw table gives the survey questions in a consistent order. This extra regularity allowed us to automatically flag questions that did not match the prior year’s phrasing, and evaluate those question fits by hand. The final product is a spreadsheet where each column is a particular year and each row tracks a question’s evolution over time. For instance, one row traces the various phrasings of “Percent; MARITAL STATUS - Males 15 years and over” in each survey. This sheet was used to standardize each question’s phrasing to the phrasing used in the final year that question appears, enabling a clean merge of the 10 years of ACS survey results.

Given the extended challenges presented by cleaning and formatting both datasets, we decided to generally stick to our initial project question: how well can we predict crime level in a city from demographic data? Our exploratory data analysis (EDA) was used as an opportunity to sanity check our data to make sure that the measures were consistent (e.g. no drastic 1000-fold changes year-over-year) to ensure that any changes we see represent real world effects and not reporting artifacts. Further, initial EDA didn’t show any obvious patterns in how specific demographics related to murder rates, which suggested that our model would likely be more useful in prediction rather than in testing inferential hypotheses.

3) Literature Review/Related Work: This could include noting any key papers, texts, or web- sites that you have used to develop your modeling approach, as well as what others have done on this problem in the past. You must properly credit sources.

Violent crime is a pervasive sociopolitical issue in the United States, especially among large metropolitan areas relative to smaller cities and rural areas[3]. And it might be getting worse. According to the New York University’s Brennan Center for Justice, while the overall crime rate increased by just 0.3% in the 30 largest American cities in 2016 relative to 2015, their murder rate went up by 14%. This large increase in murder rates was mostly led by a spike in violent crime in cities like Chicago and Charlotte[4].

Given the deleterious societal effects of crime, it is important to identify the factors associated with its increase and pervasiveness so that these may be addressed and crime be mitigated. Some of the reasons suggested by the NYU Brennan Center to explain increases in violent crime in large urban areas like Chicago are falling police numbers, poverty and other forms of socioeconomic disadvantage, and gang violence. On the other hand, studies focusing on crime levels across social networks have argued that ‘social contagion’ might be a much more powerful predictive tool than demographics alone. For example, a study on gun violence in Chicago found that many shootings were associated with retaliation and revenge attacks between gangs, causing shooting after shooting in a seemingly endless cycle. In this context, being associated with a specific gang makes you more likely to be involved in criminal activity than belonging to a specific race or gender[5].

Some have started to use data to not just describe criminal trends, but actually predict them. The existence of large amounts of police data on criminal activity over the years and the application of machine learning algorithms offer the appealing idea of predicting where and when criminal activity might occur, and who might be involved in it. In fact, Time magazine named “predictive policing” one of the top 50 ideas of 2011, and a series of cities across the United States have since implemented predictive policing software in their local police forces[6]. For example, PredPol, one of these predictive policing softwares, promises to “use artificial intelligence to help you prevent crime by predicting when and where crime is most likely to occur, allowing you to optimize patrol resources and measure effectiveness”.

However, attempting to predict crime based on police records comes with several challenges. Perhaps the largest one is the human bias associated with these data[7]. If policing efforts gravitate towards individuals and communities with specific racial and socioeconomic backgrounds, more criminal activity will be found and reported among those groups. If those skewed reports are fed to a machine learning algorithm, the model will learn those patterns and predict that those groups are more likely to commit crime, further reinforcing these biases.

Keeping in mind the promise and limitations of predicting crime based on the available criminal and demographic data over the years, we set out to build a machine learning model using as much information as we could to test our model’s predictive power.

                        

                                

                                                                

4) Modeling Approach and Project Trajectory: Include 1) a baseline model for comparison and 2) a description of your implementations beyond the baseline model. Briefly summarize any changes in your project goals or implementation plans you have made along the way. These changes are a natural part of any project, even those that seem the most straightforward at the beginning. Because of time restrictions on the project, our model was scoped to the level of a pilot analysis: a quick-and-dirty attack to measure how well a model might perform and determine if further improvement would be cost-effective. For instance, this effort could show that the model performs adequately and can be put into production, that the model is unlikely to meet operational needs even with lots of effort, or that additional improvements are likely to make meaningful improvement in the model and should be funded/staffed.

See the data section, above, for decisions regarding data cleaning. However, note that this analysis treated each city-year combination as a unique row in the dataset. That is, Albany in 2016 and Albany in 2017 are considered two distinct data points. Structuring with one row per city-year discards the time series nature of the data and is disastrous for inferential analyses and especially estimates of variability, where each row is typically assumed to be an independent draw from a population. However, correlations among rows in the data set are not an issue in a strictly predictive analysis, as conducted here. The nature of the data may reduce our predictive power relative to a similar number of truly independent observations, but there are no assumptions violated by using correlated observations.

The model arbitrarily focused on murder rate as the dependent variable. This was motivated largely by the problem specification suggesting murder prediction as a potential project goal. The other data on crime rates were dropped [see later].

After cleaning, several columns in our dataframe had large amounts of missing data and were therefore dropped. For instance, partway through the ACS the categories for mortgage costs shifted, so data from early years had e.g. $200-299 and later years had $250-399. In line with our focus on simple analysis these columns were dropped, rather than reconciled. We set an arbitrary cutoff at 100 missing values.

In addition to the dropped columns, rows with a missing dependent variable were dropped. This totaled to only 8 out of 3283 rows and should not impact the results.

The remaining missing data (in both training and test) were imputed as the mean of that column in the training data. We selected mean imputation, despite its flaws, mainly because it is deterministic and would not require additional development time estimating stochastic error based on the particular values imputed in each missing position.

Because many of our predictors report the percentage of a city’s population falling in a certain category, we selected PCA as a mechanism to combat high collinearity. Since there were substantially more rows than predictors in our analysis, we were not especially concerned with whether the PCA reduced the number of dimensions in the data. Both training and test data were standardized by the training set means and SDs before conducting the PCA transformations. The PCA weightings learned on the training data were applied to both training and test datasets.

Our baseline model was (implicitly) a model that always predicted the mean training set murder rate. This model necessarily achieves some non-positive R^2 score on the test set, and our implemented models are compared against this model’s best-possible R^2 score of 0.

We selected Lasso regression as a competing model. It serves as a more robust alternative to ordinary least-squares regression, and was arbitrarily selected over ridge regression because a particular team member thinks L1 is more intuitive than L2. Lasso’s distinction of setting more coefficients to zero than ridge regression is not especially relevant; we are not concerned with the overall number of predictors so being able to drop a set of predictors with coefficient zero isn’t a meaningful draw.

To set parameters for the PCA and lasso regression, we utilized a 5-fold cross validation on the training data, testing each possible parameter combination. This revealed a very wide mode with many nearly-optimal parameter values, so we took the observed maximum values without considering variability, as it is likely that there will be little difference in performance between the observed maximum and the surrounding values.

After a trial run with all predictors in the model, we revised the code to drop all non-murder crime statistics as explanatory variables. Although we expected this to drastically decrease model performance, as the other crime variables are highly correlated with murder rate, the model’s CV performance remained essentially constant.

Following the pilot analysis, we needed to include a technique not covered in class. From an analysis of the lasso model residuals it was clear that the heteroskedastic, normally distributed error assumptions of lasso/OLS did not hold for this dataset. Because the murder rate data are ultimately a number of murders and a total population in a metropolitan statistical area we fit a binomial regression model to the same predictors. This model performed nearly identically to the lasso model, with a small degree of overfitting because regularization is not yet implemented for binomial regression models in python.

Because our project plan was carefully considered, there were very few deviations in the modelling trajectory. However, the reader should be aware of the many choices the researchers made in preparing the data used in the analysis.

5) Results, Conclusions, and Future Work: Show and interpret your results. Summarize your results, the strengths and short-comings of your results, and speculate on how you might address these short-comings if given more time.

Our models aimed to predict murder rates using the available demographic data for all Metropolitan Statistical Areas for the period of 2007 through 2016.

The lasso regression model reached an R^2 score of .49, and average (absolute) test set residual of 1.58 murders per 100,000 individuals. The binomial regression model did not have an R^2 score implemented, but scored an average absolute test set residual of 1.61 murders per 100,000 individuals. These are surprisingly good fits for an aggregate model in the social sciences, and although we endeavored to find an error (e.g. leaving in other crime data as a predictor) we have not yet found any mistakes.

We must note that our analysis made several ad-hoc decisions in order to reach pilot model results quickly, including our handling of missing values in explanatory variables, and our particular model form.

The analysis chose to discard the time-series nature of the data, but this decision is extremely defensible. First, each city has only 10 data points in its time series; estimating any parameters would require some amount of pooling among cities, and the approach taken here is similar to a complete pooling of all cities. It is true, however, that our analysis additionally pools over years (while keeping year as a regressor) and thus discards anything but an across-the-board linear trend in murder rates from year to year. While there may be additional gains from relaxing to [pooled] quadratic or auto-regressive/moving average in the murder rate trends over time, these would add substantial complexity to the model and there is little evidence for anything more complex than a linear trend over the time period considered.

The chosen path of imputing each missing value as the column’s training set mean is plainly sub-optimal. Mean imputation suppresses variance in each predictor and sets the missing value without regard to the values of the remaining predictors. Mean imputation, therefore, makes a very strong assumption about independence and shrinkage. Bootstrap imputation, where each missing value is drawn IID from the observed values in that column, relaxes the shrinking while a model-based imputation could begin to relax the independence assumption. Since this work focused on a pilot prediction, we spent no effort on understanding how missing data came about. With more time a detailed investigation to repair the missingness could meaningfully improve the model’s performance.

The implemented model did not include any non-linear terms in the lasso regression, again favoring speed and simplicity over the potential gains of a complex model. The linearity assumption fails in two places: first, it is possible that quadratic or other transformations of the input variables would yield better predictive power. Second, the response variable, murder rate per 100,000 individuals has upper and lower bounds. A binomial regression model would be more appropriate, as it would prevent estimates of negative crime rates or crime rates above 100 percent and was implemented next.

Finally, the binomial regression model did not include any regularization penalty due to a deficient implementation in sklearn and statsmodels. This results in a slightly overfit binomial regression model, with the overfitting likely to become worse as more predictors are explored. For the sake of simplicity, the binomial model adopted the optimal PCA level found in the lasso model, but could benefit from a separate cross-validation to search for a PCA threshold that also cuts down on the number of parameters and helps combat overfitting.

CONCLUSION

All in all, and in line with the goals of our project, our models provide a good first approach to using extensive demographic data to predict murder rate. As noted above, we are aware of the aspects of our models that need more work and improvement, but we are also excited about the results we are already observing, and look forward to further data cleaning and model fine-tuning opportunities.
